Ubuntu is a Linux distribution derived from Debian and composed mostly of free and open-source software. Ubuntu is officially released in multiple editions: Desktop, Server, and Core for Internet of things devices and robots. The operating system is developed by the British company Canonical[18] and a community of other developers, under a meritocratic governance model.

As with other Linux distributions, all of the editions can run on a computer alone, or in a virtual machine. An upgrade to Ubuntu is released every six months, with long-term support (LTS) releases every two years.[8][20][21] Canonical provides security updates and support for each Ubuntu release, starting from the release date until the release reaches its designated end-of-life (EOL) date.[8][22][23] Canonical generates revenue through the sale of premium services related to the Ubuntu software and donations from those who download Ubuntu directly.[24][25][26]

Ubuntu is named after the Nguni philosophy of ubuntu, “humanity to others” with a connotation of “I am what I am because of who we all are”. Since the release of the first version in 2004, Ubuntu has become one of the most popular Linux distributions for general purposes and is backed by large online communities like Ask Ubuntu. Numerous community-editions of Ubuntu also exist. It is also popular for cloud computing, with support for OpenStack.

Pros: Ubuntu is one of the most user-friendly Linux distributions, offering a polished and consistent experience, which makes it an ideal choice for beginners and those seeking a hassle-free setup. Its extensive community support, regular updates, and large software repository ensure that users can easily find help and install a wide variety of applications.

Cons Although Ubuntu is highly accessible, its default configuration, which uses Snap and APT package management systems, can sometimes feel restrictive or inconsistent for advanced users who prefer more control over their environment. Additionally, its frequent release cycle and larger footprint can make it less suitable for lightweight or highly customized setups.

Software management: Ubuntu uses the APT package manager
